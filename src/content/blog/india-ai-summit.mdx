In February 2026, I attended the India AI Summit in New Delhi. The room had Demis Hassabis, Dario Amodei, Nandan Nilekani, Vinod Khosla, Alexander Wang, and a mix of policymakers and founders building for India.

Here's what I took away.

## AI infrastructure in India is moving faster than most people realise

India has 5G in most villages. UPI processes more digital transactions than any country on earth. Aadhaar covers 1.4 billion people. The digital public infrastructure layer already exists. The conversation at the summit wasn't "how do we build infrastructure" but "what do we build on top of it."

## The talent bottleneck is real

Every speaker mentioned it. India produces a lot of engineers but very few who can build at the frontier. The gap isn't access to information. It's access to mentorship, hard problems, and environments that push you. This is something I've seen firsthand at IIT Delhi and Imperial.

## Indic language AI is still far behind

Models work well in English. They're 3-4x worse in Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, and most Indian languages. This isn't just a technical problem. It means hundreds of millions of people are locked out of the tools being built right now. Multiple speakers flagged this as the biggest unsolved problem for AI in India.

## The government is paying attention

This wasn't a tech conference where policy is an afterthought. Senior IAS officers, ministry secretaries, and NITI Aayog members were in the room alongside founders. The Digital India stack (Aadhaar, UPI, DigiLocker) shows what happens when government and technology align. The next wave is AI on top of that stack.

## What it crystallised for me

I'm going back to India after graduating. The problems here are hard, the scale is massive, and the infrastructure to build on is better than most outsiders think. The summit made that conviction sharper.
